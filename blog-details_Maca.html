THE HISTORY OF THE DISTRICT OF MACA

The ancestors tell that the place where the district of Maca is now was a giant lagoon, however, there were already settlements on the banks of the lagoon. In one of the expeditions of the Inca Mayta Capac, he found this lagoon and saw that near it, there was a deep ravine (colca canyon). With his sling, he threw a stone at a mountain so that the water could be directed towards the ravine. After this, the Inca withdrew to his domain. Little by little, the lagoon was drying up and the inhabitants perceived that during the nights something shone in the center of the lagoon. They waited for the lagoon to dry out completely before approaching it, where they found two sisters. The villagers were amazed and at the same time scared, to such an extent that they wanted to cut them off to see if they were human or not but they didn't. The sisters were very wise and worked miracles in the district of Maca. At the end of their lives, the sisters were named patron saints of the place and today their anniversary is celebrated every year, this being one of the most important festivities. One of the most remembered miracles by the locals occurred in the 17th century, when a small fire started inside the church. Nobody noticed what happened until the next morning when the villagers realized that one of the patron saints had disappeared. Years later, the same virgin was found in the district of Espinar-Cuzco.

ORIGIN OF THE NAME OF THE DISTRICT OF MACA

Time after the population has increased in the place, the Inca Mayta Capac passed again by the place where previously it was a lagoon. As was customary, the Inca was invited to drink the representative drink of the Inca Empire, "the chicha"; this was very ripe or fermented. Some inhabitants drank in excess and the effect they had was similar to that of alcoholic beverages. This caused two villagers to have an argument in front of the Inca, upon seeing this, Mayta Capac said "Maccanakunco" (fight). From this phrase originates the name of the district, which originally was Macca meaning "to fight". After some years, it was changed to Maca.
